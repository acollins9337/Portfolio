VEXU Robot "Meowth"

The goal of this project was to create a robot specifically for the Skills (individual robot) challenge of the VEXU SpinUp game, where robots had to launch discs into goals, much like frisbee golf. Robots were to obtain the discs from the ground and were not allowed to hold more than three at any given time. Much of my rolewas to design and test mechanical components and ensure smooth integration with the programming team. My team successfully qualified for the VEX Robotics World Championships this year, along with our head-to-head match sister teams.

Hardware

Most robot designs this year used flywheel launcher systems, because they could be loaded and launched quickly, with less emphasis on accuracy and more emphasis on volume. Since the skills challenge had a lower time limit (1 minute) and did have the concerns about defense from an opposing team, accuracy was paramount. We decided to use a linear slider launcher to maintain high accuracy and keep disc groupings tight.

The primary issue with the linear launcher method was how to avoid motor back spin. A direct linkage would require the launcher elastics to have to spin the motors in reverse to launch, slowing launch velocity and potentially damaging the electronics. Many similar catapult style systems used slip-gears, or gears missing teeth, but this did not give us fine control over when we launched the disks. We instead decided on a pneumatic lever release system, which could physically separate the gears connecting the motor to the winch. Doing so gave us fine control over the launch while avoiding motor back spin. Any issues with re-meshing the gears could be avoided by having gears with large teeth.

The intake system was a linked horizontal intake system. This allowed the robot to easily pick up the flat discs from the floor and deposit them directly into the basket of the linear launcher. Multiple tiers of intake were created, such that the lowest tiers spun the fastest, and the higher tiers spun the slowest, to prevent overshooting the basket while the robot was moving.

Sensing and Programming

All Vex V5 motors come preequipped with encoders, which we supplemented with an IMU. We implemented odometry and PD control to ensure movement accuracy when performing autonomous runs. Since VEX fields are relatively small and VEX's GPS sensors are generally unreliable, autonomous runs relied on dead reckoning to move around. The launcher mechanism was soft-limited to a paricular draw distance in the code to prevent over drawing. Various preset draw distances were allowed so human operators could quickly select how much power they wanted to use.
